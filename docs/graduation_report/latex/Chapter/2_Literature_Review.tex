\section{Motivation}\label{motivation}

Fungal identification from colony images is difficult because appearance varies across \gls{media}, camera angle, lighting, and colony maturity~\cite{zielinski2020deep}. In this thesis, the target is not only to classify known samples, but to retrieve biologically plausible neighbors for a held-out \gls{strain}. A retrieval-first design is therefore preferable to a closed-set classifier.

The practical motivation is threefold:

\begin{itemize}
\item New reference data can be added by embedding and indexing new segments in \gls{qdrant}; full model retraining is not required for every new \gls{strain}.
\item Strain-level hold-out evaluation is closer to laboratory usage than image-level random splits, because the system must generalize to unseen \glspl{dto}.
\item Retrieval exposes nearest-neighbor evidence, which is more interpretable for mycologists than a single opaque class score.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}\label{methodology}

\subsection{Overall Retrieval Pipeline}\label{overall-retrieval-pipeline}

The research workflow is organized as a staged pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
\item segmentation of Petri-dish images into colony crops,
\item feature extraction from each crop,
\item supervised fine-tuning of deep backbones,
\item vector indexing and nearest-neighbor retrieval in \gls{qdrant},
\item threshold analysis for known-vs-unknown rejection.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,keepaspectratio]{figures/query_flow.png}
\caption{Overall fungal retrieval research pipeline from dish image to final species prediction.}
\label{fig:overall-retrieval-pipeline}
\end{figure}

The final species prediction is produced by aggregating evidence across multiple colony segments extracted from the same input dish. The experiments then vary the segmentation method, feature extractor, retrieval aggregation strategy, and threshold formula to determine which configuration is most robust.

\subsection{K-Means Segmentation Methodology}\label{kmeans-segmentation-methodology}

The K-Means segmentation experiment standardizes each input dish to a deterministic preprocessing pipeline before colony extraction~\cite{macqueen1967kmeans}.

\subsubsection{Stage 1 --- Full-Image Preprocessing}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Resizing}: Images are standardized to $(256 \times 256)$ pixels to ensure consistent input dimensions.
\item \textbf{Circle Detection / Plate Interior Model}: The pipeline uses a fixed plate-interior radius of 112 pixels at 256 resolution, centered on the resized image, to approximate the Petri-dish boundary.
\item \textbf{Background Masking}: Pixels outside the detected dish are masked to black, removing labels, shadows, and bench background.
\item \textbf{Cropping}: The dish region is retained as the working image for downstream segmentation.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Stage 2 --- Colony Segmentation}

The current production-style K-Means path uses HSV-based foreground separation, followed by morphology and colony-level refinement. The most important observation is that color-based segmentation is sensitive to flare: when agar around a small bright colony becomes overexposed, K-Means tends to absorb the flare into the foreground, producing a bounding box that is wider than the true colony.

\subsubsection{Stage 3 --- Local Foreground Refinement}

To reduce the flare error, a local shrink step is applied around candidate colony regions. This refinement is effective in many cases, but it still depends on the color distribution of the local patch and can fail when the bright halo is visually similar to the colony itself.

\subsection{YOLO Segmentation Methodology}\label{yolo-segmentation-methodology}

The YOLO segmentation experiment trains a learned instance-segmentation model instead of relying on color clustering~\cite{redmon2016yolo,yolov8_ultralytics}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Dataset preparation}: COCO-exported manual annotations are converted into a YOLO segmentation dataset.
\item \textbf{Split generation}: A deterministic train/test manifest is written with seed 42; the current experiment records train and test counts in split metadata.
\item \textbf{Model}: YOLOv8n-seg is used by default.
\item \textbf{Training configuration}: 50 epochs, image size 640, batch size 16, patience 10.
\item \textbf{Artifacts}: training outputs include the best checkpoint weights saved for later inference.
\end{itemize}

The key benefit of YOLO is that it learns a shape-and-context prior for the true colony boundary. This improves the edge cases where agar flare is too bright for pure color clustering. In those cases, YOLO better preserves the real colony extent while avoiding the overly wide foreground mask sometimes produced by K-Means.

\subsection{Feature Extraction Methodology}\label{feature-extraction-methodology}

Feature extraction converts each segmented colony image into an embedding vector. The study evaluates both hand-crafted descriptors~\cite{dalal2005hog,ouhanoun1998gabor,haralick1973texture} and deep backbones~\cite{he2016resnet,tan2019efficientnet,sandler2018mobilenetv2}.

\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}p{3cm}X X@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Family} & \textbf{Examples} & \textbf{Role} \\
\midrule
Hand-crafted & HOG, Gabor, RGB histogram, HS histogram & Low-cost texture and color baselines \\
Deep pretrained & ResNet50, MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB1 & Transfer-learning baselines \\
Deep fine-tuned & ResNet50, MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB1 & Main retrieval candidates \\

\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

All embeddings are normalized before indexing so cosine similarity can be used consistently inside \gls{qdrant}.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning Methodology}\label{fine-tuning-methodology}

Deep backbone fine-tuning, evaluated via architectures like ResNet~\cite{he2016resnet} and EfficientNet~\cite{tan2019efficientnet}, uses supervised classification as a proxy task. Two training paths are available: general backbone fine-tuning using cross-entropy classification, and YOLO-crop fine-tuning on colony crops derived from the YOLO segmentation dataset.

For the strongest fold-specific EfficientNetB1 setting documented in the notebook workflow, the training configuration is batch size 16, 50 epochs, patience 10, and cross-entropy loss. A triplet-loss variant is also present in the codebase, but it underperformed relative to supervised classification.

\subsection{Retrieval Methodology}\label{retrieval-methodology}

The retrieval experiment uses the Qdrant vector database~\cite{qdrant} to index all database segments after embedding extraction; queries are then compared against that index with metadata filtering and score aggregation~\cite{cover1967knn}.

The main retrieval strategies currently present in code are:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{weighted}: score-weighted aggregation,
\item \textbf{uni}: uniform vote aggregation,
\item \textbf{relative}: score share relative to competing species,
\item \textbf{per\_species\_avg} and \textbf{max\_score}: analysis baselines.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Validation Methodology}\label{cross-validation-methodology}

Shared strain-level cross-validation uses 5-fold round-robin assignment. This is the evaluation pattern applied consistently across experiments.

\begin{itemize}
\item Five folds are loaded from fold manifest files generated from the prepared segments metadata.
\item Each fold holds out one \gls{strain} per \gls{species}.
\item For every fold, the pipeline trains or selects the model configuration, indexes the database into \gls{qdrant}, and queries held-out test groups.
\item During retrieval, the query \gls{strain} is explicitly excluded using the \texttt{exclude\_strain} filter.
\item Sibling filtering also removes neighbors from the same parent image.
\end{itemize}

This design ensures evaluation reflects true generalization to unseen strains rather than memorization of near-duplicate segments.

\subsubsection{a) Setup}

Backbones were trained with supervised classification and then reused as embedding encoders. The strongest documented EfficientNetB1 fold workflow used 50 epochs, batch size 16, patience 10, and cross-entropy loss.

\subsubsection{b) Results}

The training curves in Figure~\ref{fig:training-curves} show stable convergence, and the best EfficientNetB1 fine-tuned model outperformed both pretrained baselines and the triplet-loss variant.

\subsubsection{c) Findings}

Cross-entropy fine-tuning is the right default for this dataset scale. The triplet-loss path remains useful as a negative result, showing that metric-learning objectives alone were not enough to overcome limited data volume.

The cross-validation results and heatmaps (Figure~\ref{fig:cv_heatmaps}) confirm that freq\_strength aggregation at small $K$ values generalizes best, with E2 + freq\_strength + $K=3$ achieving 0.829 mean accuracy. The cross-validation heatmaps show E2 environment (all-growth-media retrieval) outperforming E1 (same-medium) for most aggregation strategies, and freq\_strength consistently dominating weighted, uni, and relative across all $K$ values.

Confusion matrix analysis (Figures~\ref{fig:cv_confusion_best_cases} and~\ref{fig:cv_confusion_extremes}) confirms representation learning is the dominant accuracy driver: the best fine-tuned model achieves high recall across all species, while the worst fine-tuned configuration (typically a medium-exclusion strategy) drops to near-random classification on several species. The best traditional extractor (color histogram) performs moderately on some species but fails completely on visually similar pairs.

A species-level inspection of the best fine-tuned confusion matrix reveals that \emph{Penicillium aurantiogriseum}, \emph{P. neoechinulatum}, \emph{P. polonicum}, and \emph{P. viridicatum} are perfectly recovered across all 30 test-set predictions each. The major remaining failure mode is \emph{P. cyclopium}, which is consistently confused with \emph{P. freii} and \emph{P. neoechinulatum}. This makes cyclopium the primary target for future data expansion or specialized metric-learning work.

\subsection{Threshold Experiments}\label{threshold-experiments}

\subsubsection{a) Setup}

Threshold analysis computes known-vs-unknown discrimination from top-k retrieval scores~\cite{bendale2016openset,kareinen2024opensetPlankton} using formula families evaluated with three threshold-selection algorithms: F1-grid optimization, ROC optimization, and Otsu's method. In the corrected pipeline, the so-called \emph{diverse} set is the low-quality incoming dataset (`new\_data`), which contributes 58 strains expanded into 348 grouped test sets (6 test sets per strain: segment 0--2 $\times$ ob/rev). Known classes are detected dynamically by matching incoming species names against the curated database after stripping the ``Penicillium'' prefix.

\subsubsection{c) Findings}

The threshold stage now runs on the corrected incoming dataset and produces a best F1 of 0.4072 using the \texttt{exp\_decay\_15} formula with F1-grid thresholding. The key signal is real but imperfect: known test sets have higher top-1 scores than unknown ones, yet the overlap is substantial. After normalizing species labels, the known-class retrieval accuracy shows strong performance on \emph{P. freii} and \emph{P. polonicum} but persistent failure on \emph{P. melanoconidium} and \emph{P. neoechinulatum}. Thresholding therefore extends retrieval from closed-set ranking to open-set screening, but its quality remains bounded by the underlying retrieval confusion structure.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

The updated research pipeline supports a coherent conclusion. First, segmentation quality materially affects downstream retrieval because boundary errors propagate into every later stage~\cite{colonyYOLO2025,silva2025dlColony}. Second, fine-tuned EfficientNetB1 is the best fixed feature backbone and should remain the default for subsequent work, as shown in the extractor comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:extractor_comparison}). Third, the shared 5-fold strain-level cross-validation design is a major strength of the experimental methodology because it indexes the database per fold, excludes the same strain during search, and evaluates on unseen biological isolates rather than near-duplicate images. The cross-validation results (Table~\ref{tab:cv_best_configs}) and heatmaps (Figure~\ref{fig:cv_heatmaps}) show freq\_strength aggregation at small $K$ achieving highest mean accuracy, confirming robust generalization after fine-tuning~\cite{fan2020fungiAttention}.

Finally, YOLO segmentation is not a wholesale replacement for K-Means in every case; instead, it is the better solution for the bright-flare edge cases where color clustering overestimates the colony foreground. This makes YOLO the stronger candidate when annotation budget is available and boundary fidelity matters most.
