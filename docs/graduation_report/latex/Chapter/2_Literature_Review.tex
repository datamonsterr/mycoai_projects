\section{Motivation}\label{motivation}

Fungal identification from colony images is difficult because appearance varies across \gls{media}, camera angle, lighting, and colony maturity~\cite{zielinski2020deep}. In this thesis, the target is not only to classify known samples, but to retrieve biologically plausible neighbors for a held-out \gls{strain}. A retrieval-first design is therefore preferable to a closed-set classifier.

The practical motivation is threefold:

\begin{itemize}
\item New reference data can be added by embedding and indexing new segments in \gls{qdrant}; full model retraining is not required for every new \gls{strain}.
\item Strain-level hold-out evaluation is closer to laboratory usage than image-level random splits, because the system must generalize to unseen \glspl{dto}.
\item Retrieval exposes nearest-neighbor evidence, which is more interpretable for mycologists than a single opaque class score.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}\label{methodology}

\subsection{Overall Retrieval Pipeline}\label{overall-retrieval-pipeline}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,keepaspectratio]{figures/ch02_research_pipeline.png}
\caption{Overall fungal retrieval research pipeline: six-stage algorithm flow from dish image preprocessing through colony segmentation, feature extraction, Qdrant vector indexing, nearest-neighbor retrieval with aggregation, and threshold-based known-vs-unknown species prediction.}
\label{fig:overall-retrieval-pipeline}
\end{figure}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 1 --- Preprocessing.} Raw Petri-dish images undergo center-cropping to square, resizing (1024~px working, 256~px export), and automatic plate boundary detection via Hough Circle Transform. Pixels outside the detected dish are masked to black, removing labels, shadows, and bench background. The masked dish image is the single input to segmentation.

\noindent\textbf{Stage 2 --- Colony Segmentation.} The masked dish is converted to HSV color space and clustered with KMeans ($K=11$). The foreground cluster is selected by scoring each cluster on its 95th-percentile Value and 75th-percentile Saturation within the dish interior. Morphological operations (close $\rightarrow$ erode $\rightarrow$ dilate) clean noise; contours yield a bounding box per colony. A local $K=2$ shrink step tightens boxes around bright colony cores to correct the flare artifact. Output: a list of colony bounding boxes.

\noindent\textbf{Stage 3 --- Feature Extraction.} Each colony crop is passed through a feature encoder. Two families are evaluated: \emph{deep} (EfficientNetB1, ResNet50, MobileNetV2 --- both ImageNet-pretrained and fine-tuned on fungal colonies) and \emph{classical} (Color Histogram HSV, HOG gradient orientation, Gabor filter bank). Each encoder produces a fixed-length feature vector per colony segment.

\noindent\textbf{Stage 4 --- Vector Indexing.} Feature vectors are upserted into a \gls{qdrant} collection with named vectors per extractor type. Payload metadata per point includes species, strain, growth medium, camera angle, and segment index. The collection supports independent queries across different feature extractors without duplicating payload data.

\noindent\textbf{Stage 5 --- KNN Retrieval and Aggregation.} A query image passes through the same preprocess $\rightarrow$ segment $\rightarrow$ extract pipeline. Qdrant returns $K$ nearest neighbors by cosine similarity. The environment strategy (same-media E1 or all-media E2) filters the search space. Query-strain siblings are excluded to simulate unseen-isolate evaluation. Votes from $N$ colony segments are aggregated using one of eight strategies: \texttt{freq\_strength}, \texttt{weighted}, \texttt{uni} (unweighted), \texttt{relative}, \texttt{perquery\_norm\_avg}, \texttt{per\_species\_avg}, \texttt{max\_score}, or \texttt{rank\_product}. Output: ranked species list with confidence scores.

\noindent\textbf{Stage 6 --- Threshold Analysis.} The top-$K$ neighbor scores are passed to a threshold formula that maps them to a scalar confidence value. Three threshold-selection algorithms (F1-grid, ROC optimization, Otsu's method) determine the optimal cut-point. Confidence $\geq$ threshold labels the prediction as a known species; confidence $<$ threshold rejects it as unknown or foreign. The pipeline is evaluated by accuracy, F1, precision, and recall.

\subsection{K-Means Segmentation Methodology}\label{kmeans-segmentation-methodology}

The K-Means segmentation experiment standardizes each input dish to a deterministic preprocessing pipeline before colony extraction~\cite{macqueen1967kmeans}.

\subsubsection{Stage 1 --- Full-Image Preprocessing}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Resizing}: Images are standardized to $(256 \times 256)$ pixels to ensure consistent input dimensions.
\item \textbf{Circle Detection / Plate Interior Model}: The pipeline uses a fixed plate-interior radius of 112 pixels at 256 resolution, centered on the resized image, to approximate the Petri-dish boundary.
\item \textbf{Background Masking}: Pixels outside the detected dish are masked to black, removing labels, shadows, and bench background.
\item \textbf{Cropping}: The dish region is retained as the working image for downstream segmentation.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Stage 2 --- Colony Segmentation}

The current production-style K-Means path uses HSV-based foreground separation, followed by morphology and colony-level refinement. The most important observation is that color-based segmentation is sensitive to flare: when agar around a small bright colony becomes overexposed, K-Means tends to absorb the flare into the foreground, producing a bounding box that is wider than the true colony.

\subsubsection{Stage 3 --- Local Foreground Refinement}

To reduce the flare error, a local shrink step is applied around candidate colony regions. This refinement is effective in many cases, but it still depends on the color distribution of the local patch and can fail when the bright halo is visually similar to the colony itself.

\subsection{YOLO Segmentation Methodology}\label{yolo-segmentation-methodology}

The YOLO segmentation experiment trains a learned instance-segmentation model instead of relying on color clustering~\cite{redmon2016yolo,yolov8_ultralytics}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Dataset preparation}: COCO-exported manual annotations are converted into a YOLO segmentation dataset.
\item \textbf{Split generation}: A deterministic train/test manifest is written with seed 42; the current experiment records train and test counts in split metadata.
\item \textbf{Model}: YOLOv8n-seg is used by default.
\item \textbf{Training configuration}: 50 epochs, image size 640, batch size 16, patience 10.
\item \textbf{Artifacts}: training outputs include the best checkpoint weights saved for later inference.
\end{itemize}

The key benefit of YOLO is that it learns a shape-and-context prior for the true colony boundary. This improves the edge cases where agar flare is too bright for pure color clustering. In those cases, YOLO better preserves the real colony extent while avoiding the overly wide foreground mask sometimes produced by K-Means.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning Methodology}\label{fine-tuning-methodology}

Deep backbone fine-tuning, evaluated via architectures like ResNet~\cite{he2016resnet} and EfficientNet~\cite{tan2019efficientnet}, uses supervised species classification as a proxy task. The pre-trained backbone (ImageNet) is loaded with its original weights, then the classification head is replaced with a Dropout(0.3)-Linear sequence for our 8 fungal species classes. The model is trained end-to-end with \texttt{AdamW} (learning rate $10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) on the strain-holdout YOLO segment split defined in Section~\ref{cross-validation-methodology}.

\subsubsection{Backbone Export and Feature Extraction}

After training, the classification head (\texttt{model.fc} for ResNet50, \texttt{model.classifier} for MobileNetV2 and EfficientNetB1) is discarded. Only backbone parameters (convolutional layers, batch normalization, residual blocks, and squeeze-excitation modules) are exported as a \texttt{.pth} weight file. The feature extractor then loads these exported backbone weights, replaces the final classification layer with an identity mapping, and passes each segmented colony image through the truncated network. The output is an L2-normalized embedding vector of dimension 2048 (ResNet50) or 1280 (MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB1).

\subsubsection{Augmentation Policy}

Training augmentations combine spatial and photometric transformations to improve generalisation: \texttt{RandomResizedCrop} (scale $[0.75,1.0]$, ratio $[0.9,1.1]$), \texttt{RandomHorizontalFlip}, \texttt{RandomRotation} ($\pm10^\circ$), and \texttt{ColorJitter} (brightness $\pm0.2$, contrast $\pm0.2$). All images are resized to $224\times224$ and normalised with ImageNet statistics before batching.

\subsubsection{Training Configuration}

All three architectures share the same training protocol: strain-level holdout split, batch size 16, up to 25 epochs with early-stopping patience of 15, and cross-entropy loss. The AdamW optimizer provides decoupled weight decay ($10^{-4}$), and Dropout (0.3) precedes the classification head.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Finetuning setup summary --- per-model layer customisation:}
\vspace{-0.3em}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{ResNet50}: Load \texttt{resnet50(weights=IMAGENET1K\_V1)} $\rightarrow$ replace \texttt{model.fc} with \texttt{Sequential(Dropout(0.3), Linear(2048, 8))}. All parameters unfrozen; classifier head discarded after training.
\item \textbf{MobileNetV2}: \texttt{mobilenet\_v2(weights=IMAGENET1K\_V1)} $\rightarrow$ replace \texttt{classifier[1]} with \texttt{Sequential(Dropout(0.3), Linear(1280, 8))}. Full fine-tuning; \texttt{classifier} keys excluded at weight export.
\item \textbf{EfficientNetB1}: \texttt{efficientnet\_b1(weights=IMAGENET1K\_V1)} $\rightarrow$ replace \texttt{classifier[1]} with \texttt{Sequential(Dropout(0.3), Linear(1280, 8))}. Full fine-tuning; \texttt{classifier} keys excluded at weight export.
\item \textbf{Weight export}: For ResNet50, filter keys starting with \texttt{fc.}; for MobileNetV2/EfficientNetB1, filter keys starting with \texttt{classifier.}. Saved as \texttt{\{ModelName\}\_finetuned.pth}.
\item \textbf{Feature extraction}: Load exported backbone weights $\rightarrow$ set classifier to \texttt{Identity()} $\rightarrow$ forward pass $\rightarrow$ L2-normalize output (dim 2048 or 1280).
\item \textbf{Why unfreeze all}: Domain gap (ImageNet$\rightarrow$ fungus colonies) is large; early features benefit from adaptation. Without regularization, training accuracy reaches 96\% while validation plateaus at 52\% --- overfitting. Dropout + weight decay closes this gap.
\item \textbf{Segment input size}: $224\times224$, normalised with ImageNet $\mu/\sigma$.
\item \textbf{Data split}: 7 test strains (one per species $\times$ 7 media $\times$ 2 angles $\times$ 3 segments = 42 test predictions), 996 training / 294 validation colony segments from YOLO segmentation.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Why We Unfreeze All Layers}

When fine-tuning on a small fungal dataset (996 training segments, 8 species), partial freezing (training only the last few convolutional blocks) produced weaker results than full fine-tuning. The domain gap between ImageNet scenes and close-up colony images is substantial enough that early-layer feature extractors benefit from adaptation. However, without regularisation, full fine-tuning on ResNet50 reached 96\% training accuracy while validation accuracy plateaued at 52\%, indicating severe overfitting. Adding Dropout and AdamW regularisation simultaneously improved validation accuracy to 55.8\% and yielded retrieval features that outperformed the pretrained baseline.

\subsubsection{Contrast with YOLO-Crop Fine-Tuning}

The older YOLO-crop fine-tuning path (\texttt{train\_yolo\_crops.py}) freezes most backbone layers and trains only the last block plus the classification head. This was effective when crops were larger and training data was partitioned differently, but under the current strain-holdout design with YOLO segment inputs, full fine-tuning with regularisation is the preferred configuration.

\subsection{Retrieval Methodology}\label{retrieval-methodology}

The retrieval experiment uses the Qdrant vector database~\cite{qdrant} to index all database segments after embedding extraction; queries are then compared against that index with metadata filtering and score aggregation~\cite{cover1967knn}.

Let a strain be represented by $M$ query segment images $\{q_1,\dots,q_M\}$. For each query $q$, $\gls{qdrant}$ returns $K$ nearest neighbors from the database. Define the following per-species accumulations across all queries:

\begin{align}
N_x &= \{(q,j) \mid \text{neighbor }j\text{ of } q \text{ belongs to species } x\} \label{eq:neighbor_set}\\
C_x &= |N_x| \label{eq:count}\\
W_x &= \sum_{(q,j) \in N_x} c_j \label{eq:weight}\\
Q_x &= \{q \mid \exists j \text{ such that } (q,j) \in N_x\} \label{eq:query_set}
\end{align}

where $c_j \in [0,1]$ is the cosine similarity returned by $\gls{qdrant}$. Let $T = \sum_x C_x$ be total known-neighbor count, $W = \sum_x W_x$ be total score mass, and $M$ be the number of query images. The eight aggregation strategies are:

\begin{enumerate}[font=\bfseries,label=\textbf{\arabic*.}]
\item \textbf{weighted} (default): $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{W_x}{T}$.\\
Fraction of total neighbor score-mass belonging to species $x$. Susceptible to dilution when similar species split the neighbor pool---values rarely exceed $0.5$.

\item \textbf{uni}: $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{C_x}{T}$.\\
Fraction of neighbor \emph{counts} (ignores similarity magnitude).

\item \textbf{relative} (recommended for ranking): $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{W_x}{W}$.\\
Share of total raw-evidence sum. All species scores sum to $1$; top species approaches $1.0$ when it dominates the neighbor pool.

\item \textbf{per\_species\_avg}: $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{W_x}{C_x}$.\\
Mean cosine similarity for species $x$. Natural $[0,1]$ range; a single outlier neighbor can inflate a rare species.

\item \textbf{max\_score}: $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \max_{(q,j) \in N_x} c_j$.\\
Single best match across all queries; ignores prevalence across the neighbor set.

\item \textbf{perquery\_avg}: $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{q=1}^M \frac{W_{x,q}}{K}$.\\
Per-query sum divided by $K$, then mean across queries. Differs from weighted when unknown neighbors are present.

\item \textbf{perquery\_norm\_avg}: $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{q=1}^M \frac{W_{x,q}}{\sum_{y} W_{y,q}}$.\\
Normalize each query's per-species scores to sum to $1$ (soft allocation), then average. Equal voter per query.

\item \textbf{freq\_strength}: $\displaystyle \mathrm{score}_x = \frac{|Q_x|}{M} \cdot \frac{W_x}{C_x}$.\\
Appearance frequency $\times$ average match strength. Favours species that appear consistently across queries \emph{and} match with high similarity. This strategy produced the strongest cross-validation performance.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Cross-Validation Methodology}\label{cross-validation-methodology}

Shared strain-level cross-validation uses 5-fold round-robin assignment. This is the evaluation pattern applied consistently across experiments.

\begin{itemize}
\item Five folds are loaded from fold manifest files generated from the prepared segments metadata.
\item Each fold holds out one \gls{strain} per \gls{species}.
\item For every fold, the pipeline trains or selects the model configuration, indexes the database into \gls{qdrant}, and queries held-out test groups.
\item During retrieval, the query \gls{strain} is explicitly excluded using the \texttt{exclude\_strain} filter.
\item Sibling filtering also removes neighbors from the same parent image.
\end{itemize}

This design ensures evaluation reflects true generalization to unseen strains rather than memorization of near-duplicate segments.

\subsection{Threshold Experiments}\label{threshold-experiments}

\subsubsection{a) Setup}

Threshold analysis evaluates known-vs-unknown species discrimination by querying held-out incoming strains against a full reference database and applying a threshold formula to the top-k retrieval scores. The experiment tests whether a retrieval system deployed in the laboratory can flag a novel strain that has no match in the reference collection~\cite{bendale2016openset,kareinen2024opensetPlankton}.

The retrieval engine uses the \texttt{qdrant-research} \gls{qdrant} collection containing segment embeddings from 8 curated species and 31 strains (the \texttt{original\_prepared} dataset). Only species present in the curated reference collection are indexed in the database; incoming diverse strains are never added to the index. This ensures that an unknown strain cannot match itself in the database. Each incoming test strain is divided into 6 test sets (3 K-means colony segments $\times$ 2 camera angles, from \texttt{full\_prepared} dataset), producing $58 \times 6 = 348$ grouped retrieval samples. For each test set, EfficientNetB1\_finetuned extracts features from every colony crop and queries \gls{qdrant} with $K=11$ same-environment neighbors. The top-1 species from weighted aggregation is used as the prediction, and the top-5 score vector is passed to the threshold stage.

Known/unknown ground truth is determined by matching the incoming species name against the curated database after stripping the ``\emph{Penicillium}'' prefix. Of the 58 diverse strains, 5 have known species matches with available query segments (48 test sets covering \emph{P. freii}, \emph{P. melanoconidium}, \emph{P. neoechinulatum}, \emph{P. polonicum}, and \emph{P. tricolor}); the remaining 51 strains (300 test sets) include 3 additional Penicillium species without segment data plus 48 non-Penicillium species — all treated as unknowns requiring threshold rejection.

\begin{figure}[ht]

\subsubsection{c) Findings}

The threshold experiment on the \texttt{qdrant-research} collection (7 curated reference species, 31 strains, 1,290 indexed segments) demonstrates three key findings:

\textbf{Known-species retrieval accuracy.} After normalizing species labels by stripping the ``\emph{Penicillium}'' prefix, the known-class accuracy reaches 66.7\% (32/48 test sets correctly classified). Species-level results are uneven: \emph{P. polonicum} is strongest at 12/12 (100\%), \emph{P. freii} reaches 10/12 (83.3\%), \emph{P. tricolor} reaches 9/12 (75.0\%), \emph{P. neoechinulatum} reaches 1/6 (16.7\%), and \emph{P. melanoconidium} remains the hardest case at 0/6. This pattern suggests that the current embedding space separates some colony morphologies well, but still collapses several related taxa.

\textbf{Open-set detection signal.} The top-1 retrieval score $s_0$ carries a visible but still limited separation signal between known and unknown strain groups. The regenerated results give a mean $s_0$ of 0.195 for known test sets versus 0.143 for unknowns, yet the two distributions still overlap heavily. Using all top-5 scores is therefore necessary, but the improvement remains modest: the best regenerated formula is \texttt{gap\_0\_2} with ROC-based threshold selection, reaching F1 = 0.3409, precision = 0.2344, recall = 0.6250, and specificity = 0.6667.

\textbf{Scale and generalization.} The refreshed threshold evaluation contains 342 grouped query samples: 48 known and 294 unknown. The confusion matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:threshold_confusion}) shows that unknown strains are still absorbed by a few dominant reference classes, especially \emph{P. polonicum} and \emph{P. tricolor}. This is expected for nearest-neighbour retrieval without explicit rejection, and it explains why thresholding remains mandatory for laboratory deployment. The present result should therefore be treated as a conservative baseline rather than a solved open-set detector.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

The updated research pipeline supports a coherent conclusion. First, segmentation quality materially affects downstream retrieval because boundary errors propagate into every later stage~\cite{colonyYOLO2025,silva2025dlColony}. The latest K-means vs.\ YOLO comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:kmeans-vs-yolo}) using the same fine-tuned ResNet50 backbone yields identical retrieval accuracy (0.810) for both segmentation methods, confirming convergence on comparable colony boundaries. YOLO's advantage is concentrated in the bright-flare edge case described in Section~\ref{kmeans-segmentation-methodology}.

Second, fine-tuned deep backbones now consistently outperform their ImageNet-pretrained counterparts under the canonical weighted/E1/$K=7$ protocol (Table~\ref{tab:finetune-results}). ResNet50 (FT 0.810 vs. PT 0.714), MobileNetV2 (FT 0.786 vs. PT 0.643), and EfficientNetB1 (FT 0.762 vs. PT 0.548) all show positive deltas. The key enablers were Dropout(0.3) before the classification head and AdamW with weight decay $10^{-4}$, which together prevented the severe overfitting (96\% training vs. 52\% validation) observed in earlier full-fine-tuning runs.

Third, the eight aggregation strategies remain a complete design space for post-retrieval evidence fusion. The empirical ordering is clear: \texttt{freq\_strength} $>$ \texttt{weighted} $>$ \texttt{relative} $>$ \texttt{uni} across all extractor families and environment configurations. \texttt{freq\_strength} at small $K$ achieves the best cross-validation accuracy (E2, $K=3$: 0.829 mean accuracy).

Fourth, 5-fold cross-validation confirms E2 (all-media) retrieval outperforms E1 (same-media) for most strategies, demonstrating that multi-medium indexing provides more robust evidence. Fold-specific training curves (Figure~\ref{fig:cv_training_curves}) show consistent convergence within 10--15 epochs across architectures.

Finally, the threshold pipeline (ResNet50 finetuned, same as the top retrieval backbone) produces a reproducible known-vs.-unknown baseline: best F1 = 0.364 (\texttt{avg\_top5}, \texttt{w\_equal}), known-class accuracy = 0.583, precision = 0.229, recall = 0.896. Unknown species still gravitate toward dominant reference classes (\emph{P. polonicum}, \emph{P. tricolor}), confirming that explicit threshold rejection remains mandatory for laboratory deployment. The staircase chart (Figure~\ref{fig:threshold_staircase}) tracks 576 formula-algorithm combinations, converging at F1 $\approx$ 0.36.
